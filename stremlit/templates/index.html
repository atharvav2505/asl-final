Learning Modules

Lesson 1: Basic Fingerspelling

Learn to spell your name and common words using the ASL alphabet. Fingerspelling is the foundation of ASL communication and is used for proper nouns, technical terms, and when there isn't a specific sign for a word.

Lesson 2: Common Greetings

Master essential greeting signs including "Hello", "Good morning", "How are you?", and "Nice to meet you". These are the building blocks of everyday conversations in ASL.

Lesson 3: Numbers and Counting

Learn to sign numbers 1-100 and understand the importance of palm orientation. Numbers are crucial for expressing quantities, dates, times, and ages in ASL.

Lesson 4: Family and Relationships

Discover signs for family members, friends, and relationship terms. Learn how to describe your family structure and important people in your life.

Lesson 5: Everyday Vocabulary

Build your vocabulary with common words for daily activities, food, places, and emotions. This module covers over 100 essential signs for everyday communication.

About American Sign Language

What is ASL?

American Sign Language (ASL) is a complete, natural language that has the same linguistic properties as spoken languages, with grammar that differs from English. It is expressed by movements of the hands and face.

ASL is the primary language of many deaf North Americans, and it is one of several communication options used by people who are deaf or hard-of-hearing. Like any spoken language, ASL is a language with its own unique rules of grammar and syntax.

History of ASL

1817

The American School for the Deaf was founded in Hartford, Connecticut, marking the formal beginning of ASL in the United States.

1960s

William Stokoe published groundbreaking research proving that ASL is a true language with its own grammar and syntax.

1990

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was signed into law, significantly improving accessibility for deaf individuals.

Present Day

ASL is recognized as a legitimate language and is taught in schools and universities across the United States. Technology continues to improve accessibility.

Key Features of ASL

Visual-Spatial Language: ASL uses space, movement, and facial expressions to convey meaning, making it uniquely suited for visual communication.

Grammar Structure: ASL has its own grammar that is different from English, including topic-comment structure and the use of classifiers.

Facial Expressions: Facial expressions are grammatically crucial in ASL, not just emotional. They can change the meaning of signs and indicate questions, negations, and more.

Regional Variations: Like spoken languages, ASL has regional dialects and variations across different communities.

Why Learn ASL?

Learning ASL opens doors to communication with the deaf community, enhances cognitive abilities, provides career opportunities in interpretation and education, and promotes inclusivity and understanding across communities.

With over 500,000 deaf individuals in the United States and many more who are hard of hearing, learning ASL is a valuable skill that can bridge communication gaps and foster meaningful connections.
